课程简介：
本课程为英语专业研究生专业课，课程选取奥斯汀、勃朗特、詹姆斯、乔伊斯、菲茨杰拉德、格雷厄姆·格林、石黑一雄、麦克尤恩等经典作家的代表性作品，与其跨媒介的电影改编或反哺文本进行精读与课堂讨论。课程以“小说—影视输出”与“影视—小说输入”两大板块为轴线，系统学习英美文学与电影互动的历史与美学特征，并结合法国意大利电影传统与日本文学电影文本，探讨跨国与跨媒介叙事的可能性。教学内容涵盖以下层次：体验文本与影像、理解叙事与媒介、阐释改编与互文、比较批评与文化分析。要求学生在阅读经典文学与观摩相关电影的同时，参考理论文献与批评著作，并能够在比较研究的基础上提出具有原创性的学术见解与思考。 课程共16周，分为三个阶段：1.导论与方法论（Week 1）：介绍课程目标、研究方法与理论框架。2.小说—电影改编案例研究（Week 2–8）：以英美经典小说及其影视改编为核心，兼顾法、意、日等跨国电影传统，探讨改编的忠实性、创新性与文化政治。3.电影—小说反哺及跨媒介实验（Week 9–15）：以石黑一雄、村上春树、麦克尤恩等为案例，研究当代文学对电影叙事、影像美学的吸纳与重写。4.总结与研究成果展示（Week 16）：学生汇报课程论文计划，进行反思与讨论。
英文课程简介：
In the twenty-first century, media convergence intensifies this exchange, raising philosophical questions about the identity of any single work once it circulates across novel, film, and television. Against this historical and theoretical background, the seminar turns to an intensive case study: the fiction of Kazuo Ishiguro. Ishiguro’s novels are uniquely suited to examining the interaction of text and image. Marked by restrained prose, unreliable narrative, ellipses, and ambiguity, his works invite adaptation while themselves are sometimes adaptations from cinematic forms. The seminar focuses on three novels: A Pale View of Hills (1982), The Remains of the Day (1989), and Never Let Me Go (2005). Each will be studied alongside its major screen adaptation(s), and cinematic inspirations including the 1943 war film The Life and Death of Colonel Blimp directed by Michael Powell. Through sustained close reading of prose and films, we will examine how narrative techniques migrate across media and most importantly, how cinema not only becomes a form of critical commentary but also serves as a major source from which novel adapts. For even adaptation is adaptable. By situating Ishiguro within a century-long dialogue between fiction and film, the course demonstrates how adaptation clarifies—not diminishes—the artistic identity of a work. In tracing the movement of these novels across media, we confront two larger questions that animate the seminar: when a story appears in multiple forms, what remains constant, what changes, and how do those transformations reshape our understanding of narrative itself? When a story is written alongside a film, or inspired by a film, how do these developing changes change our understand of the novel?
